What LLMs Overlook Compared to the Human Eye

For this assignment, I wanted to test a simple question: do visuals speak for themselves? To explore this, I took three visualizations from my Harry Potter corpus and gave them to an LLM with no context at all. I asked it to explain the visuals and then interpret them. What happened was interesting the LLM sounded confident, but it often made assumptions that were not actually in the visuals. This helped me see how important context is when we use computational text analysis. It also connected strongly to two readings from class: Lisnic’s article about misleading charts and Glyndarkin’s piece about how LLMs “actually read.” Together, they helped me understand why the LLM kept over‑interpreting my charts.

1. Word Cloud: A Simple Visual That Still Misleads

<img src="{{ '/assets/images/image3.jpg' | relative_url }}" alt="Color-coded word cloud">

Visual 1
Figure 1. Word cloud comparing five Harry Potter texts.
When I gave the LLM the word cloud, it correctly explained the basics: bigger words mean higher frequency, and the colors show which text each word came from. But when it started interpreting the visual, it went far beyond what the chart actually shows. It said the texts were “character driven,” that fanfiction “keeps the same relationships,” and that emotional tone could be seen from words like voice or looked. None of this is in the word cloud. The chart only shows word frequency, not themes or emotions.
This connects to Lisnic’s point that most misleading charts are not misleading because of design mistakes, but because people make reasoning errors when they look at them (Lisnic). The LLM did the same thing: it filled in missing information with assumptions based on what it already knows about Harry Potter. This shows that even a simple visual like a word cloud needs context, or people and LLMs will read too much into it.

2. Bubblelines: More Complexity, More Assumptions
<img src="{{ '/assets/images/image4.jpg' | relative_url }}" alt="Color-coded word cloud">
Visual 2
Figure 2. Bubblelines showing word frequency patterns across texts.
The Bubblelines chart was harder for the LLM, and its mistakes became even clearer. It explained the layout correctly, each row is a text, each bubble is a word, and bubble size shows frequency. But when it interpreted the chart, it started making claims about “emotional peaks,” “story climaxes,” and “narrative pacing.” The chart does not show any of that. It only shows where certain words appear.
This is exactly what Lisnic describes as “incorrectly inferring causality” one of the most common reasoning errors people make when reading charts (Lisnic). The LLM saw clusters of words like love or fear and assumed they represented emotional moments in the story. But the chart does not include chapters, plot points, or emotional labels. The LLM simply guessed.
Glyndarkin’s reading helped me understand why this happens. He argues that LLMs “actually read” everything we give them, even when the information is incomplete (Glyndarkin). So when the Bubblelines chart didn’t give enough context, the LLM tried to build a full story out of it. This shows how easily a complex visual can be misunderstood without proper framing.

3. Heatmap: Raw Numbers Without Context
<img src="{{ '/assets/images/image2.jpg' | relative_url }}" alt="Color-coded word cloud">
Visual 3
Figure 3. Heatmap showing word frequencies across texts.
The heatmap produced the clearest example of why context matters. The LLM explained the chart correctly, but its interpretation was misleading. It said the original books were “more emotional” because words like dead and love appeared more often. But the heatmap uses raw counts, and my texts are very different lengths. One text has over 170,000 words, while another has only 23,000. Without normalizing the data, you cannot compare emotional tone.
This connects again to Lisnic’s argument that misleading interpretations often come from missing context, not from the chart itself (Lisnic). The LLM treated the heatmap as if all texts were equal in size, which led to incorrect conclusions.
Glyndarkin’s point about LLMs reading everything also applies here. The LLM tried to make sense of the numbers, even though the chart didn’t give enough information to support its claims. It filled in the gaps with assumptions, just like people do online.

Reflection: What I Learned About Visuals and Context
After doing this experiment, I realized that visuals never fully speak for themselves. Even when a chart looks simple, people and LLMs bring their own assumptions to it. Lisnic shows that most misleading charts online are not “bad charts” they are charts that people interpret incorrectly because they lack context. Glyndarkin explains that LLMs read everything deeply, which means they will also over interpret visuals if we don’t guide them.
My LLM did exactly that. It made confident claims that were not supported by the visuals. This helped me see that when I use visuals in my own writing, I need to explain them clearly and give the right context. Otherwise, readers human or machine might misunderstand what the data actually shows.
